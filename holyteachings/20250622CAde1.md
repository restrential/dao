道大理微妙見佛時花開

身示道範德風.法衍聖脈儒宗
報恩了愿貫始終.至誠如神感通

善導能仁.文行信忠

普施時雨沃甘霖.受享鴻恩致光榮

調寄：楚留香新傳
語寄：亨

### 仙佛集結

五美：
1. 惠而不費
2. 勞而不怨：不抱怨
3. 欲而不貪：求仁而得仁
4. 泰而不驕：舒泰
5. 威而不猛

公生明，廉生威

### Allusion Notes

**我執 (wǒ zhí)**
*Source: Buddhist Terminology (Sanskrit: ātma-grāha)*
The fundamental Buddhist concept of grasping at a permanent, inherent self. Buddhism holds this delusion to be the root of suffering. In 我執成障礙, it is not external obstacles but this inner ego-clinging that becomes the obstruction to perceiving truth. The verse makes the teaching immediately personal: the blockage is inside us.

**如來 (rú lái)**
*Source: Buddhist Terminology (Sanskrit: Tathāgata)*
One of the ten primary epithets of a Buddha, meaning "Thus Come One" — one who has arrived from and returned to the ultimate. In 疏通見如來, clearing ego-grasping reveals the Buddha-nature already present, or opens perception to the enlightened reality. The Tathagata is not attained from outside; the path is removal of obstruction.

**浩然正氣 (hào rán zhèng qì)**
*Source: 孟子 (Mencius), Gongsun Chou I (公孫丑上)*
Mencius describes this as "supremely great and firm" — a vast, flowing moral energy cultivated through an unbroken accumulation of upright action. It cannot be forced or performed; it grows from the inside out. 滿懷浩然正氣 — to be filled with this qi — signals that the guide's moral integrity is not a posture but an organic overflow.

**至誠如神 (zhì chéng rú shén)**
*Source: 禮記 (Book of Rites), 中庸 (Doctrine of the Mean)*
The original states that one of utmost sincerity possesses an almost divine prescience — not supernatural ability but such perfect alignment with natural principle that good and ill fortune become perceptible before they manifest. Paired here with 感通 (moves and connects), the line teaches that sincere cultivation creates a living channel to the spiritual world.

**參贊天地造化功 (cān zàn tiān dì zào huà gōng)**
*Source: 禮記 (Book of Rites), 中庸 ch. 22*
The Zhongyong teaches that a person of utmost sincerity can fully develop their own nature, then the nature of others, then the nature of all things — and thereby assist in the transforming and nourishing work of Heaven and Earth. This is the summit of the Zhongyong's arc. Its appearance here places the teaching's ambition at the highest possible register: not personal virtue but cosmic co-creation.

**齊聖廣淵 (qí shèng guǎng yuān)**
*Source: 左傳 (Zuo Zhuan), Duke Wen, 18th Year*
This phrase originally described the four cardinal virtues of Emperor Shun: 齊 (solemn and composed), 聖 (wise and perceptive), 廣 (magnanimous and broad), 淵 (profound and deep). As an exemplar of the ideal sage-ruler, Shun's qualities here become the standard for anyone who 秉公 — upholds impartial justice in guiding others.

**能仁 (néng rén)**
*Source: Buddhist Terminology — epithet of Shakyamuni Buddha*
能仁 (Able-Compassionate One) is one of the liturgical names for Shakyamuni Buddha, translating the Sanskrit Śākyamuni's underlying meaning of "capable/able sage." 善導能仁 is therefore not just "skillfully guide with benevolence" but specifically "guide in the manner of the Buddha" — compassionate skill rooted in the Able One's example. This synthesis of Confucian 善導 with Buddhist 能仁 is characteristic of the Dao tradition's integration.

**文行信忠 (wén xíng xìn zhōng)**
*Source: 論語 (Analects), 述而 (Shu Er)*
Confucius's four subjects of instruction: 文 (culture and learning), 行 (conduct), 忠 (wholehearted commitment), and 信 (trustworthiness). They form the complete curriculum of character cultivation. Their appearance here alongside 善導能仁 suggests the Buddhist and Confucian paths are not alternatives but parallel tracks of the same work.

**五美 (wǔ měi)**
*Source: 論語 (Analects), 堯曰 (Yao Yue)*
In the final chapter of the Analects, Zizhang asks Confucius about governance. Confucius explains the Five Beauties (五美) and Four Evils (四惡) of leadership. The Five: (1) 惠而不費 — generous without extravagance; (2) 勞而不怨 — assign labor without causing resentment; (3) 欲而不貪 — have desire without greed; (4) 泰而不驕 — be dignified without arrogance; (5) 威而不猛 — be awe-inspiring without being ferocious. These principles of just governance become in the 仙佛集結 a template for spiritual cultivation and community leadership.

**亨嘉會 (hēng jiā huì)**
*Source: 易經 (Book of Changes), Hexagram 2 (坤 Kūn — The Receptive)*
亨 is the term for success and unobstructed passage in the Yijing. 嘉會 means a beautiful, auspicious gathering. Combined with 合禮中 — coming together within the bounds of propriety — the line describes an assembly that is both fortunate and rightly ordered: when the gathering is authentic and conducted with proper reverence, it succeeds.

**雲集景從 (yún jí jǐng cóng)**
*Source: 漢書 (Book of Han), Jia Yi's "Faults of Qin" (過秦論)*
The original: "天下雲集響應，贏糧而景從" — the realm gathered like clouds, responded like echoes, carried food and followed like shadows. Jia Yi described the popular momentum behind a movement. Here, 愷惻雲集景從 inverts the political register: a person of genuine kindness and compassion naturally draws followers — not by power but by the gravitational pull of authentic warmth.

**不改其操 (bù gǎi qí cāo)**
*Source: 論語 (Analects), 雍也 (Yong Ye)*
The allusion is to Confucius's praise of Yan Hui: in a lane with a single bamboo vessel of rice and gourd of water — conditions others could not bear — Yan Hui 不改其樂 (did not change his joy). Here 操 (moral principles / integrity) replaces 樂, deepening the image: it is not merely contentment but one's fundamental character that must remain unaltered under difficulty.

**清明在躬 (qīng míng zài gōng)**
*Source: 禮記 (Book of Rites), 孔子閑居 (Kongzi at Home at Ease)*
Full line: "清明在躬，氣志如神" — when clarity and brightness are within the person, the qi and will become like a spirit's. Inner lucidity — a mind unfogged by desire, prejudice, or distraction — empowers a perceptive will. The phrase describes the quality of the person, not an external achievement.

**誾誾如 (yín yín rú)**
*Source: 論語 (Analects), ch. 10:2*
From the chapter describing Confucius's demeanor: "與下大夫言，誾誾如也" — when speaking with lower ministers, he was 誾誾: dignified, frank, and harmoniously measured. This quality of speech — calm, firm, and elegant without sharpness — is the register in which the teaching's guidance should be received and transmitted.

**永言配命 (yǒng yán pèi mìng)**
*Source: 詩經 (Book of Odes), Major Odes, "King Wen" (大雅·文王)*
Original context: King Wen's conduct was forever in alignment with Heaven's Mandate. He did not grasp the mandate — he embodied it through ceaseless vigilance and virtue. 永言配命 therefore means not merely "obey a decree" but to so thoroughly align one's being with Heaven's will that obedience and nature become identical.

**繼志述事 (jì zhì shù shì)**
*Source: 禮記 (Book of Rites), 中庸 (Doctrine of the Mean)*
Defined as the highest filial piety: "善繼人之志，善述人之事" — being good at carrying on the aspirations of one's predecessors and good at continuing their work. In the context of Dao lineage, 繼志 means carrying the vision of the sages forward, and 述事 means continuing the practical work they began. This is how the sacred cause is perpetuated.

**公生明，廉生威 (gōng shēng míng, lián shēng wēi)**
*Source: Ming Dynasty official maxim (官箴)*
A famous inscription for government offices: impartiality (公) produces clarity of sight; incorruptibility (廉) produces genuine authority. The maxim appears at the end of the teaching as a summary principle: those who lead — in governance or in spiritual community — earn their clarity and authority not by assertion but by these two inner qualities.

## Research Summary — Gleaned Insights

- **The Five Beauties (五美) are the structural core**: The 本訓 names the 五美 explicitly in line 6, and the 仙佛集結 expands on them with explanatory notes. The entire teaching is essentially an exhortation to embody these five Analects principles in daily cultivation and community leadership. All other allusions orbit this center.

- **Ji Gong's distinctive register**: As 濟顛和尚 — the eccentric Chan monk Ji Gong — the teacher addresses disciples as 徒兒 (children/little ones) and calls himself 爾師 (your teacher), a warmth absent in more formal teachings. The tune 楚留香新傳 (New Legend of Chu Liu Xiang) adds a heroic, chivalrous color: Ji Gong is the knight-errant of the spiritual world, unconventional and compassionate, whose moral authority comes from action and sacrifice, not rank.

- **能仁 is a Buddhist name for Shakyamuni, not a generic adjective**: 善導能仁 means "guide with the compassion of the Able-Compassionate One (the Buddha)." This is not generic benevolence — it invokes the Buddha explicitly as the model for guiding others, completing the line's integration of Confucian skill (善導) with Buddhist compassion (能仁).

- **Zhongyong's arc from inner sincerity to cosmic participation**: 至誠如神感通 → 參贊天地造化功 follows the Zhongyong's central movement exactly: inner sincerity → connecting with the divine → assisting in the transforming work of Heaven and Earth. This is not a distant aspiration but the natural consequence of sustained cultivation.

- **齊聖廣淵 (Zuo Zhuan) invokes Emperor Shun's four virtues as the standard for leadership**: Those who 秉公 (uphold impartiality) must embody all four: solemn, wise, broad, profound. These are not individual traits but a composite portrait of the ideal guide-leader.

- **誾誾如 (Analects 10:2) describes a quality of dignified measured speech**: Often overlooked, this term from the behavioral chapter of the Analects (describing Confucius's own demeanor) sets the standard for how cultivation should manifest in the quality of one's speech and presence — not just what is said but how one carries oneself in discourse.

- **公生明，廉生威 closes as a governance maxim applied to cultivation**: The Ming official's maxim is transposed from government office to spiritual community: the teacher earns authority (威) through incorruptibility (廉), and clear-sightedness (明) through impartiality (公). This grounds the teaching's close in practical ethics of leadership.

## Step 3: Smoothed Translation

### 鎮壇詩

Ego-attachment becomes the obstruction —
clear the way, and the Tathagata appears.
Dao is vast, its principles subtle and wondrous;
when the Buddha is seen, flowers bloom.

### 吾乃

I am your teacher, Ji Dian He Shang —
bearing Φ's command, I enter the hall.
Through the gate I bow before August Φ,
then ask my disciples: are you all well?

### 本訓

Demonstrate the Dao in person — let virtue's influence spread;
extend the teaching through the sacred lineage of the Confucian tradition.
Repay grace and fulfill vows, holding fast from beginning to end;
utmost sincerity, like a spirit, moves and connects.

Filled with vast, flowing righteous qi —
a whole heart of red-hot sincerity, ferrying the masses across.
Participate in heaven and earth's creative work;
with solemnity, wisdom, breadth, and depth, uphold impartiality.

Guide skillfully, in the way of the Able-Compassionate One;
let culture, conduct, faithfulness, and loyalty be your path.

The Five Beauties harmonize heavenly principle with human affairs;
be a model, transmit transformation, bear the heavy burden —
compassionate vow without end.

The auspicious gathering comes together in accord with propriety;
those of kind and compassionate heart gather followers like clouds,
and are followed like shadows.

Hold fast to your principles — serve with unwavering integrity;
tread the upright path, embody greatness, relieve those in distress, and connect all.
Rejoice in Dao, humble and self-emptying;
let clarity and brightness dwell within.

Dignified and measured, forever accord with the Mandate of Heaven;
reverently carry on the will and work of those who came before —
the sacred cause: pass it down, make it flourish.

Respond to all beings with earnest, sincere authenticity;
absorb transformation, vigorous as a dragon.
Bestow timely rains universally, nourishing all with sweet dew;
receive the vast grace — glory naturally follows.

*Set to the tune: New Legend of Chu Liu Xiang (楚留香新傳)*
*Addressed to: 亨 (Heng)*

### 仙佛集結

**The Five Beauties:**

1. Be generous without being extravagant.
2. Toil without complaint — hold no grievances.
3. Have desires without greed — seek benevolence, and benevolence is found.
4. Be at ease without arrogance — serene and at peace.
5. Be awe-inspiring without being ferocious.

*Impartiality gives rise to clarity; incorruptibility gives rise to authority.*

## Final Translation

### 鎮壇詩

Ego-attachment becomes the obstruction —
clear the way, and the Tathagata appears.
Dao is vast, its principles subtle and wondrous;
when the Buddha is seen, flowers bloom.

### 吾乃

I am your teacher, Ji Dian He Shang —
bearing Φ's command, I enter the hall.
Through the gate I bow before August Φ,
then ask my disciples: are you all well?

### 本訓

Demonstrate the Dao in person — let virtue's influence spread;
extend the teaching through the sacred lineage of the Confucian tradition.
Repay grace and fulfill vows, holding fast from beginning to end;
utmost sincerity, like a spirit, moves and connects.

Filled with vast, flowing righteous qi —
a whole heart of red-hot sincerity, ferrying the masses across.
Participate in heaven and earth's creative work;
with solemnity, wisdom, breadth, and depth, uphold impartiality.

Guide skillfully, in the way of the Able-Compassionate One;
let culture, conduct, faithfulness, and loyalty be your path.

The Five Beauties harmonize heavenly principle with human affairs;
be a model, transmit transformation, bear the heavy burden —
compassionate vow without end.

The auspicious gathering comes together in accord with propriety;
those of kind and compassionate heart gather followers like clouds,
and are followed like shadows.

Hold fast to your principles — serve with unwavering integrity;
tread the upright path, embody greatness, relieve those in distress, and connect all.
Rejoice in Dao, humble and self-emptying;
let clarity and brightness dwell within.

Dignified and measured, forever accord with the Mandate of Heaven;
reverently carry on the will and work of those who came before —
the sacred cause: pass it down, make it flourish.

Respond to all beings with earnest, sincere authenticity;
absorb transformation, vigorous as a dragon.
Bestow timely rains universally, nourishing all with sweet dew;
receive the vast grace — glory naturally follows.

*Set to the tune: New Legend of Chu Liu Xiang (楚留香新傳)*
*Addressed to: 亨 (Heng)*

### 仙佛集結

**The Five Beauties:**

1. Be generous without being extravagant.
2. Toil without complaint — hold no grievances.
3. Have desires without greed — seek benevolence, and benevolence is found.
4. Be at ease without arrogance — serene and at peace.
5. Be awe-inspiring without being ferocious.

*Impartiality gives rise to clarity; incorruptibility gives rise to authority.*

## Translator's Notes

1. **濟顛和尚 (Jì Diān Héshang)**: The beloved and eccentric Chan Buddhist monk known popularly as Ji Gong (濟公). Historical figure from the Southern Song dynasty; celebrated for his unconventional compassion, his habit of appearing drunk and disorderly while secretly performing miracles for the common people, and his refusal to follow monastic conventions out of a deeper, more fluid morality. In the Dao tradition he appears as a holy teacher whose authority derives from authentic compassion, not rank. His intimate forms of address — 爾師 (your teacher), 徒兒 (my little ones) — are consistent with this persona.

2. **Φ**: A reverential substitution for 天 (Heaven / the divine), used throughout White Era holy teachings. Retained as Φ in the English per established convention.

3. **皇Φ vs. 老皇Φ**: This teaching uses 皇Φ (August Φ), without the 老 (ancient/venerable) prefix found in some other teachings. Rendered as "August Φ" accordingly — not "Ancient August Φ."

4. **能仁 (néng rén)**: A Buddhist liturgical name for Shakyamuni Buddha, deriving from the underlying Sanskrit meaning of Śākyamuni ("capable/able sage") as "able benevolence." 善導能仁 synthesizes Confucian 善導 (skillful guidance) with Buddhist 能仁 (the Able-Compassionate One), characteristic of the Dao tradition's integration of the Three Teachings.

5. **浩然正氣 (hào rán zhèng qì)**: Mencius (Gongsun Chou I) describes this as "supremely great and firm," grown from an unbroken accumulation of upright action. It cannot be forced; it is organic. The translation "vast, flowing righteous qi" preserves both its magnitude and its dynamic, flowing quality.

6. **齊聖廣淵 (qí shèng guǎng yuān)**: From Zuo Zhuan (Duke Wen, 18th Year), originally describing Emperor Shun's four virtues: solemn, wise, broad, and profound. These become the character ideal for anyone who upholds impartial justice.

7. **誾誾如 (yín yín rú)**: A behavioral term from Analects ch. 10:2, describing Confucius's dignified and harmoniously measured manner of speech when engaging lower officials. Often overlooked, it signals that the quality of cultivated presence — how one carries oneself in speech — is itself a form of practice.

8. **楚留香新傳 (New Legend of Chu Liu Xiang)**: A classic wuxia novel by Gu Long, adapted as a celebrated 1984 Taiwan television series. 楚留香 is a gallant, free-spirited knight-errant known for his compassion for the weak and disregard for convention — a figure whose moral authority comes from action and sacrifice, not station. As the tune for Ji Gong's teaching, it is fitting: two unconventional heroes who work in the margins.

9. **仙佛集結 (Assembled Immortals and Buddhas Teaching Notes)**: A section following the main verse where additional notes are given by the assembled spiritual beings. Here the Five Beauties from the Analects are glossed: 勞而不怨 is explained as 不抱怨 (hold no grievances); 欲而不貪 as 求仁而得仁 (seek benevolence and benevolence is found — a reference to Analects 7:15); 泰而不驕 as 舒泰 (serene and at ease).

10. **公生明，廉生威**: A Ming dynasty maxim inscribed in government offices. Transposed here from governance to spiritual community: those who lead earn their clarity of vision through 公 (impartiality) and their authority through 廉 (incorruptibility). A fitting close that grounds the teaching's ideals in practical ethics.
